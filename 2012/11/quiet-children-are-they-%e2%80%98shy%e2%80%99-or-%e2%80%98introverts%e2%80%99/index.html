Quiet children: are they ‘shy’ or ‘introverts’?

Date posted: Monday 26th November 2012

I spend a lot of time thinking about children who are quiet, and particularly those who are unhappy about joining in during group activities. We often assume that these children are ‘shy’, but what does this mean? A good way to think about shy children is see them as really wanting to contribute through talking, but their anxiety about ‘performing’ in front of a group prevents them from doing so. This bothers them, and if adults put pressure on them to talk, this can increase their self-consciousness and create anxiety.

But some children are happy with their quietness. This may be because they have an introverted temperament. The word ‘introvert’ is often used in a negative way to describe someone who is quiet. It is also confused with being ‘shy’. They are not the same things!

I asked Maggie Johnson, co-author of the Selective Mutism Resource Manual and Supporting Quiet Children to define what she means by being ‘an introvert’. She gave this very helpful thumbnail description of the difference between shyness and introversion

The extrovert goes to the party and has a great time.
The introvert stays at home and has a great time.
The shy person desperately wants to go to the party but stays at home.

Maggie directed me to Urban Dictionary and this is what they say about Introverts (I have edited it a bit!)

Opposite of extrovert. A person who is energized by spending time alone. Often found in their homes, libraries, quiet parks that not many people know about, or other secluded places. Introverts like to think, and to be alone.
Contrary to popular belief, not all introverts are shy. Some may have great social lives and love talking to their friends, but just need some time to be alone to “recharge” afterwards. The word “Introvert” has negative connotations that suggest that these people need to be ‘changed’. Introverts are simply misunderstood because the majority of the population consists of extroverts (or people who think they should act like extroverts!)

Being an “introvert” might mean that you, or your child, may have one or more of these qualities:
One who focuses on the inner world of thoughts and ideas.
One who gains energy from ‘alone time’; one who thinks before they speak.

Susan Cain has written a fascinating book that suggests that there is nothing wrong with being an introvert. In fact, in Quiet: The Power of Introverts in a World that Won’t Stop Talking she sees it as a positive advantage, and especially in groups. Most of what we do in early years settings and schools is about functioning -and talking- in groups. We feel that we are failing if a child is not joining in, and especially in large group activities such as Circle Time.

We worry, quite rightly, if children spend time on their own. But might it be that they are introverts who are feeling just a bit overwhelmed by our busy settings and schools, and are searching for a bit of space for themselves?

We need to be careful to avoid negative labelling of children, because labels such as ‘She’s painfully shy’, ‘She’s introverted’ can be taken to heart and become part of a child’s and teenager’s self-image, that can follow them into adulthood.

So if you are the parent of a child who is having problems at school because she is quiet, check out what words are being used to describe her. Are they accurate?

If you are working with a quiet child, ask yourself, “Is this child happy about being quiet? If he seems unhappy, or anxious about talking in groups, might he be shy? Or might there be other difficulties?”

Talk with his parents, and particularly find out about how he talks at home.

And if you have always been labelled as ‘shy’ (including by yourself!) maybe you are actually an introvert. In which case Susan Cain’s book will really turn you around!
